Case 1: Chest Pain (ER)
History taking · Risk assessment · Management plan
▼
Scenario: A 55-year-old male presents to the emergency department with sudden-onset chest pain. He looks pale and sweaty.
Your Task
  • Take a focused history
  • Assess cardiovascular risk factors
  • Give a working diagnosis and management plan
Key Questions to Ask
  • Onset, character, radiation of pain?
  • Associated symptoms — sweating, SOB, nausea?
  • Risk factors — HTN, diabetes, smoking, family history?
  • Previous cardiac history or medications?
🎯 Likely Diagnosis: Acute Coronary Syndrome
🫁
Case 2: Shortness of Breath
Airway assessment · Respiratory exam · Differential diagnosis
▼
Scenario: A 30-year-old presents with sudden difficulty breathing. She appears anxious and is using accessory muscles.
Your Task
  • Assess airway and breathing first
  • Perform a focused respiratory examination
  • Give a differential diagnosis with reasoning
Differentials to Consider
  • Asthma (most likely given age)
  • Pneumonia
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Pneumothorax
  • Anxiety / hyperventilation
🩸
Case 3: Severe Anaemia
Severity assessment · Cause identification · Management
▼
Scenario: A patient presents with progressive fatigue and pallor. Hb is reported as 6.5 g/dL on FBC.
Your Task
  • Identify severity and symptoms
  • Investigate for the underlying cause
  • Plan appropriate management
Management Steps
  • Blood transfusion if Hb <7 or symptomatic
  • Iron studies, B12/folate, reticulocyte count
  • Blood film to identify cell morphology
  • Treat the underlying cause
🟢 Timed OSCE Mode
⏱️

How to Practice Timed Stations

Give yourself 5–7 minutes per station — same as the real exam

1
Introduce yourself — name, role, confirm patient identity
2
Take a focused history — use SOCRATES for pain, OLDCART for symptoms
3
Perform relevant examination — expose, inspect, palpate, percuss, auscultate
4
Give impression and plan — working diagnosis, investigations, immediate management
⚠️ Examiner tip: Always wash hands, gain consent, and maintain patient dignity — these are easy marks that students frequently miss under pressure.
⚪ Clinical Frameworks

ABCDE Approach

For any acutely unwell patient

A
Airway Check if patient can speak; look for obstruction
B
Breathing RR, SpO₂, chest movement, auscultate
C
Circulation Pulse, BP, cap refill, skin colour
D
Disability GCS/AVPU, pupils, blood glucose
E
Exposure Full body exam; temperature; look for rashes

SBAR Communication

For handovers and escalating concerns

S
Situation Who you are, who the patient is, the current problem
B
Background Relevant medical history, medications, allergies
A
Assessment Your clinical impression — what you think is happening
R
Recommendation What you need — review, investigation, medication
